Introduction — Why You Must Do This Right Now

Your AWS root user is the master key to everything: billing, all resources, and every IAM user. If it gets compromised, the attacker owns your account — full stop. Yet thousands of accounts are still protected only by a password. In this zero-theory, 100% hands-on lab, you will apply the two most critical defenses AWS gives you: a strong account-wide password policy and multi-factor authentication (MFA) on the root user. These two steps alone block 99.9% of real-world credential attacks and are mandatory for any production account or certification exam (SAA-C03, Security Specialty, etc.). Follow along — it takes less than 10 minutes and is completely reversible.

Step 1: Set a Strong Account-Wide Password Policy

  1. Log in as root → IAM Dashboard
  2. Left sidebar → Account settings

3. Scroll to Password policy → Edit

4. Choose Custom policy and enable:

  • Minimum password length: 14
  • Require at least one uppercase letter
  • Require at least one lowercase letter
  • Require at least one number
  • Require at least one non-alphanumeric character (!@#$ etc.)
  • Allow all IAM users to change their own passwords: ✓
  • Enable password expiration: 90 days (optional but recommended)
  • Prevent reuse of previous 24 passwords: ✓

5. Click Save changes

Done! Every existing and future IAM console user is now forced to meet enterprise-grade password standards.

Step 2: Activate MFA on Your Root User (The Most Important Step)

Warning

Do NOT lose your phone after this step. You can always remove the MFA device later, but while it’s active, you need it to log in as root.

  1. Top-right corner → Click your account name/ID → Security credentials
  2. Scroll to Multi-factor authentication (MFA) → Assign MFA device

3. Device name: My-iPhone (or anything you’ll recognize)

4. Choose Authenticator app (virtual MFA)

5. Click Show QR code

6. Open your authenticator app:

  • Recommended: Authy, Twilio Authenticator, or Google Authenticator
  • Scan the QR code

7. The app now shows a 6-digit code that changes every 30 seconds

8. Enter two consecutive codes when prompted (e.g., current code + next code)

9. Click Add MFA

Success! You now see your device listed under “Assigned MFA device”.

Test It Immediately

  1. Log out completely
  2. Log back in as root (use your root email + password)
  3. You will now be prompted for the MFA code from your phone
  4. Enter the current 6-digit code → you’re in!

Conclusion

In under ten minutes you eliminated the two biggest attack vectors against AWS accounts: weak passwords and password-only logins. Your root user — the crown jewel — now requires both something you know (password) and something you have (your phone). Even if your password leaks on the dark web tomorrow, attackers still can’t get in without physical access to your device. Every compliance framework (PCI, HIPAA, SOC2, ISO 27001) and every AWS Well-Architected review demands exactly what you just did. You’ve moved from “hope my password is good enough” to “my account is professionally secured.” Keep that MFA app safe, consider adding a YubiKey as backup, and never skip these steps on any new account. You now run AWS the way the pros do — secure by default, from day one.
