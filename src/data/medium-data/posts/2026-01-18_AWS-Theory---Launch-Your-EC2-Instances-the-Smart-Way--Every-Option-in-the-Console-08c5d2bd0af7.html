Introduction

Do you ever guess how to get the cheapest or most reliable EC2 instances? Stop guessing. AWS gives you six different ways right inside the console, and each one is just a few clicks away. In this blog you’ll see exactly where to click for Spot Fleets (up to 90% off), regular Spot instances, Reserved Instances, Savings Plans, Dedicated Hosts, and Capacity Reservations. In addition you can check the real screenshots of pricing history. No theory overload, just “Here’s the button, here’s what it does.”

1. Spot Instances

  • Left menu → Spot Requests → Request Spot Instances

What you’ll see first:

  • In my fresh console you won't be able to see the real history, but live pricing history per instance type & AZ, you can see the graph, that will instantly proves the savings (often 70–80% stable)

Some Key Attributes

  • Target capacity: “I want 10 instances” or “I want 100 vCPUs”
  • Interruption behavior: Terminate / Stop / Hibernate
  • Allocation strategy dropdown:

i. lowestPrice → cheapest right now

ii. diversified → spread across pools

iii. capacityOptimized / priceCapacityOptimized (2025 default & best)

  • Instance pools: pick exact types (c5.large, m5.xlarge…) or just say “at least 8 vCPUs, 16 GB RAM” and let AWS choose
  • Estimated cost: instantly shows “you’ll pay $0.156/hour for 10 instances → 73% savings”

One click → massive fleet launched and auto-managed.

EC2 Spot Instances Pricing

This graph illustrates the historical pricing of Amazon EC2 Spot Instances, which are spare compute capacity in the AWS cloud available at massive discounts compared to standard On-Demand prices.

Here is a breakdown of what the different elements of the graph represent:

1.1. The Pricing Gap

  • On-Demand Price: The solid horizontal line at the top (marked at $0.1000) represents the fixed price you would pay if you bought this instance (an m4.large) normally.
  • Spot Prices: The fluctuating colored lines at the bottom represent the actual market price for Spot instances. Notice they are significantly lower, ranging roughly between $0.03 and $0.045. This represents a savings of about 60–70%.

1.2. Availability Zones (AZs)

The different colored lines represent different data centers within a region (e.g., us-east-1a, us-east-1b).

  • Price Variation: Prices vary by AZ because supply and demand are independent in each data center.
  • Strategic Choice: According to the legend on the right, us-east-1c is currently the most expensive ($0.0440), while us-east-1a and 1b are the cheapest ($0.0326).

1.3. The “User-defined max price”

The dashed horizontal line is a critical concept in Spot instances:

  • The Bid: This is the maximum amount you are willing to pay.
  • The Interruption: Notice that toward the right side of the graph (late January), the red line (us-east-1c) rises above your dashed max price line.
  • Result: If you were running an instance in that specific zone, AWS would terminate or hibernate your instance because the market price exceeded your limit.

Finally, what i am saying is…

This graph shows that Spot instances are highly cost-effective but volatile. To use them successfully, your application must be “fault-tolerant” (able to handle interruptions) or designed to switch to a cheaper Availability Zone when your max price is exceeded.

2. Spot Fleets

When we think of a Spot Fleet, it is a smart “automated shopper” for our cloud computing. Instead of you manually hunting for the cheapest server every hour, you give the Fleet a budget and a shopping list, and it does the work for you.

2. 1. The Shopping List (Launch Pools)

Instead of saying “I want exactly one m5.large in us-east-1a,” you give the Fleet options. We might say:

  • “I need 10 servers. I’m okay with m5.large or r5.large, and they can be in any of these three data centers (AZs)." Each combination of instance type and location is a pool. The more pools you give the Fleet, the better deals it can find for you.
Spot Fleets = set of Spot Instances + (optional) On-Demand Instances

2.2. The Buying Strategies

This is where you tell the Fleet how to behave. For better understanding, I am choosing a shopping personality:

  • Lowest Price (The Bargain Hunter): “I don’t care about anything except the bill. Give me the cheapest servers available right now.”
Best for: Quick jobs that don’t mind being interrupted if the price spikes.
  • Diversified (The Risk Manager): “Don’t put all my eggs in one basket. Spread my servers across all the pools.”
Best for: Reliability. If one data center has a price spike, only a small part of your fleet shuts down.
  • Capacity Optimized (The Practical Buyer): “Find the pool that has the most ‘extra’ space so I’m less likely to be kicked off.”
Best for: Longer tasks where you really want to avoid being interrupted.
  • Price Capacity Optimized (The Smart Shopper): “Look for the pools with the most space first, then pick the cheapest among those.”
Best for: Almost everything. It’s the perfect balance of “cheap” and “stable.”

2.3. How it “Meets Capacity”

You set a target capacity (e.g., “I need 50 units of computing power”).

  • The Fleet constantly monitors the market.
  • If a server gets too expensive or AWS needs it back, the Fleet automatically “fires” that server and hires a new, cheaper one from a different pool to keep you at your target of 50.
  • If you choose, you can include a few On-Demand Instances as a “baseline” that never goes away, ensuring your app never fully goes dark.

Finally, what i am saying is…

It removes the “babysitting” way of cloud management. We can define our Max Price and your Goal, and the Fleet handles the messy work of switching servers in the background, and finally, we can save money.

3. Reserved Instances (Still Exists, But…)

Think of Reserved Instances (RIs) like signing a long-term lease on an apartment instead of staying in a hotel night-to-night. You’re telling AWS, “I promise to stay here for a year or three,” and in exchange, they give you a massive discount—sometimes up to 75% off.

3.1 The “All-In” Commitment

When you buy a Reserved Instance, you are making three specific choices:

  1. The Box: You pick exactly what you want (e.g., “I want a c5.large running Linux").
  2. The Time: You commit to 1 year or 3 years. (The 3-year deal is much cheaper, but that’s a long time in the tech world!)
  3. The Cash: You choose your payment “vibe”:
  • All Upfront: You pay for the whole year today. (Biggest discount).
  • Partial Upfront: You pay a bit now and a smaller monthly bill.
  • No Upfront: You pay $0 now, but a set monthly fee for the term.

3.2. “Still Exists, But…” (The Savings Plan Takeover)

Reserved Instances used to be the only way to save money, but they have a big flaw: they are rigid.

Imagine you buy a 3-year “lease” on a c5.large. Six months later, AWS releases the c6.large, which is faster and cheaper. With a traditional Reserved Instance, you're stuck. You own that c5 until the clock runs out.

Savings Plans (the newer alternative) are like a “store credit” instead of a lease. You just promise to spend, say, $10/hour on anything AWS compute-related. If you switch from a c5 to a c6, the discount follows you automatically.

3.3. When should you actually use RIs?

Even though Savings Plans are more popular, people still use RIs in two scenarios:

  • Capacity Reservations: If you absolutely must have a specific server available in a specific building (Availability Zone) during a regional spike, an RI can “save your seat” so no one else can take it.
  • The Marketplace: There is an “eBay for RIs.” If you only need a server for 4 months, you can sometimes buy someone else’s leftover 3rd-party RI for a bargain.

Finally, what i am saying is…

Use Reserved Instances if you are 100% certain your tech stack won’t change for years and you want to “lock in” a specific server. Use Savings Plans if you want the same discount but want the freedom to upgrade your servers whenever you feel like it.

4. Savings Plans — The Modern Replacement (Recommended for 99% of Users)

If Reserved Instances are a rigid apartment lease, Savings Plans are like a VIP Membership or a “Subscription Credit.” It is currently the “gold standard” for saving money on AWS because it removes the stress of guessing what your tech needs will look like in two years.

4.1. The “Subscription” Mindset

Instead of picking a specific server model, you just commit to a dollar amount per hour.

  • The Commitment: You say, “AWS, I promise to spend at least $10 every hour for the next year.”
  • The Reward: AWS says, “Great! For that first $10 of usage every hour, we’ll give you the deep ‘Reserved’ discount (up to 72%). If you spend more than $10, you just pay the normal price for the extra.”

4.2. Why it’s “Zero Regret”

The magic is in the flexibility. Because you committed to a dollar amount and not a specific machine, you can change your mind constantly:

  • Size Swap: Start with a small server (m5.medium), realize you need a giant one (m5.4xlarge) tomorrow? The discount follows you.
  • Family Upgrade: AWS releases a new, faster “m7” chip? You can switch from m5 to m7 instantly. The discount follows you.
  • OS Flip: Switch from Windows to Linux? The discount follows you.
  • Moving Day: Move your servers from Virginia to Tokyo? With a “Compute Savings Plan,” the discount follows you.

4.3. How to Buy (The “Two Click” Method)

AWS actually makes this incredibly easy because they want your guaranteed commitment.

  1. They look at your last 30 days of usage.
  2. They tell you: “Hey, if you commit to $8.50/hour, you’ll save $400 a month.”
  3. You click “Buy,” and that’s it. No need to manually map instances to rows in a spreadsheet.

Finally, what i am saying is…

The only “risk” is over-committing. If you promise to spend $10/hour, but then you turn off all your servers for a month-long vacation, AWS still charges you that $10 every hour. You are paying for the commitment, not the actual usage.

Create Savings Plans

→ Left menu → Savings Plans Commit to $X/hour for 1–3 years →

  • get the same ~72% discount but change instance family, size, OS, and region anytime. One page, two clicks, zero regret.

5. Dedicated Hosts — When You Need the Actual Metal

Normally, when you use AWS (EC2), you are “sharing” a physical server with other customers. You can’t see them, and they can’t see you, but you’re all living on the same hardware. With a Dedicated Host, AWS wheels a specific physical rack out and says, “This is yours. Nobody else’s data touches this metal.”

5.1. The “Nasty” License (The Oracle/Windows Problem)

Some software companies (like Oracle or older Microsoft products) are very old-school. They don’t charge you “per user”; they charge you per physical processor socket or per core in the server.

  • On a normal cloud server, you don’t know how many physical sockets there are.
  • On a Dedicated Host, you can count the physical chips. This allows you to “Bring Your Own License” (BYOL) and save millions in licensing fees.

5.2. The “Strict Parent” (Compliance & Security)

Some industries (like Banking, Government, or Healthcare) have very strict rules. Their auditors might say: “I don’t care how secure the cloud is; I want to know that your data is physically separated from everyone else’s.” A Dedicated Host gives you the “Physical Isolation” certificate to keep the auditors happy.

5.3. Total Control

Because you own the whole box, you can control exactly how your virtual machines are placed on it. You can ensure two parts of your app stay on the same physical chip to make them run a tiny bit faster.

Think of Dedicated Hosts as the difference between renting a room in a hotel versus buying the entire building.

Normally, when you use AWS (EC2), you are “sharing” a physical server with other customers. You can’t see them, and they can’t see you, but you’re all living on the same hardware. With a Dedicated Host, AWS wheels a specific physical rack out and says, “This is yours. Nobody else’s data touches this metal.”

Finally, what i am saying is…

It’s a “license and compliance” play — if you don’t specifically need it for legal or software contract reasons, you’re just overpaying for a box you’ll likely leave half-empty.

Create Dedicated Hosts

→ Left menu → Dedicated Hosts → Allocate Dedicated Host

  • Pick instance family (r5, c6gn, etc.) + AZ → you now own the entire physical box. Perfect for Oracle, Windows Server BYOL, or strict compliance.

6. Capacity Reservations — “Keep 10 m5.2xlarge Warm for Me”

Think of a Capacity Reservation like pre-ordering a table at the busiest restaurant in town on New Year’s Eve. Even if the restaurant is packed and people are being turned away at the door, your table is sitting there empty, waiting just for you.

6.1. The “Peace of Mind” Reservation

Normally, AWS feels infinite. But during massive global events (like the Super Bowl, Black Friday, or a major government launch), an entire region can actually run out of a specific server type.

A Capacity Reservation is your insurance policy:

  • The Guarantee: You tell AWS, “I need 10 m5.2xlarge servers in us-east-1a starting at 8:00 AM."
  • The “Reserved Seat”: AWS sets those 10 physical slots aside. No one else can take them.
  • The Use Case: You use this for mission-critical launches where if the server doesn’t start, your business fails.

6.2. The “Ouch” Factor (The Catch)

This is where the restaurant analogy really hits: You pay for the reservation even if you don’t show up.

  • If you reserve 10 servers and only turn on 2, you still pay for all 10.
  • You aren’t paying for “usage”; you are paying for the guarantee that the hardware is there.

6.3. How it differs from “Reserved Instances”

This is a common point of confusion:

  • Reserved Instances (RI) / Savings Plans: These are about saving money. You might buy an RI but still find that the server you want is “temporarily unavailable” during a massive spike.
  • Capacity Reservations: These are about availability. There is no “discount” here. You pay the full On-Demand price just to make sure you can actually turn the machine on.

Finally, what i am saying is…

You are locked into a specific Availability Zone, so if you reserve capacity in us-east-1a but your auto-scaling group tries to launch in 1b, you’ll end up paying for the reservation and the new servers at the same time.

Create Capacity Reservations

→ Left menu → Capacity Reservations → Create capacity reservation Reserve exact count & type in a specific AZ → guaranteed availability, pay even if idle.

  • Use only when you have a big regulated launch and can’t risk “capacity unavailable.”
