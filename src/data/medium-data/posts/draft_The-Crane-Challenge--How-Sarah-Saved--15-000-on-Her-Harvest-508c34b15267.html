The Problem That Showed Up at Dawn

Sarah stood at the edge of her farm at 5:47 AM, coffee in hand, watching the crane operator shake his head.

“Ma’am, I can lift a maximum of k pounds at once,” he said, pointing at the weight limit stenciled on the crane’s boom. “But these crop sections…” he gestured at Sarah’s neatly organized field grid, “they’re not uniform. Some sections are heavy, some are light, and honestly, some have negative weight after accounting for spoilage.”

Sarah had divided her 50-acre farm into a grid for precision agriculture. Each section had a tracked weight: positive for harvestable crops, negative for sections with pest damage or poor soil that needed remediation. She needed to load crop sections onto the crane to transport them to processing, but here was the catch:

The crane could only lift rectangular sections of the grid, and the total weight had to be ≤ k pounds.

The question that would cost or save thousands: Which rectangular section should she choose to maximize crop value while staying within the weight limit?

Understanding the Landscape

Let’s start with a simpler version. Imagine Sarah’s farm as a small 2×3 grid:

Each number represents the net weight (in hundreds of pounds) after accounting for good crops (positive) and damaged sections (negative).

The crane’s weight limit: k = 200 pounds (or 2 in our scaled units).

Question: Which rectangular section gives the maximum weight ≤ 2?

Let me show you the possibilities visually. Look at the grid above. We need to find rectangles (contiguous sections) that sum to at most 200 pounds.

Some examples:

  • Single cell [1,2]: Just the premium section = 3… but wait, 3 > 2, so this violates the limit!
  • Rectangle [0,1] to [0,2]: cells {0, 1} = 1… too light
  • Rectangle [0,1] to [1,2]: cells {0, 1, -2, 3} = 0 + 1 + (-2) + 3 = 2 ✓

That blue rectangle (columns 1–2, all rows) gives us exactly k = 2. Perfect!
