Introduction

In my previous blog, I explained the Application Load Balancer (ALB) is a smart receptionist, just like the Network Load Balancer (NLB) is a super-fast freeway. It doesn’t care about the details of your conversation. It only moves big amounts of data as fast as possible. It is operating at Layer 4 (the Transport Layer). So the NLB deals directly with raw network packets (TCP and UDP). It is built for high scale, low latency, and scenarios where every millisecond counts. When you have millions of users hitting your app at once, or you’re running a high-speed gaming server, the NLB is the engine that keeps things from crashing under the weight of the traffic.

Why the NLB is Unique

1. Extreme Speed and Performance

The NLB can handle millions of requests per second with low latency. But an ALB takes an extra fraction of a second to read the URL. The NLB simply passes the traffic through instantly. This makes it the go-to choice for real-time applications.

2. Static IPs: The Fixed Address Benefit

The NLB gives you one static IP address per Availability Zone. You can even assign your own Elastic IPs to it.

  • Real-World Benefit: If our customers need to whitelist your server’s IP in their firewalls. They can’t whitelist an ALB because its IP might change. But they can trust an NLB’s fixed address forever.

3. Support for More Protocols (TCP & UDP)

The ALB only speaks the language of the web (HTTP/HTTPS). But the NLB has the capability to speak the language of the internet infrastructure (TCP and UDP).

  • We can use TCP for databases (SQL), email (SMTP), and file transfers (FTP).
  • We can use UDP for online gaming, video streaming, and voice calls (VoIP) where speed is more important than checking if every single packet arrived perfectly.

Question 1

If my servers are constantly being deleted and replaced (autoscaling) with new ones (with new IP addresses), how does the load balancer know the new ‘IP address’ to call?

In this question, exactly many people get confused.

EC2 instances in an Auto Scaling Group change their IPs all time. However, the Static IP magic happens at the load balancer level, not the server level.

Let’s see this example

The Front Gate vs. Back Office Analogy

Imagine a big company has a static phone number (the NLB).

  • The Front Gate (NLB): This has one fixed phone number that never changes. Your customers save this number in their contacts (this is the whitelisting).
  • The Back Office (EC2 Instances): Inside the building, there are 100 desks with different employees. These employees (instances) come and go. When one employee leaves, another one sits.

Finaly what is happening: Let’s say if a customer randomly calls to that static phone number, the NLB instantly forwards the call to whoever is currently sitting at a desk in the back office. The customer never knows that the person on the other end is a new employee or that they are at a different desk.

How the NLB actually handles this:

1. The NLB is the Anchor

When you create an NLB, AWS gives fixed IP address for each Availability Zone. This IP never changes even if you delete every single EC2 instance behind it.

2. The Target Group is the Middle Man

The NLB doesn’t talk directly to the Auto Scaling Group. It talks to a target group.

  • As the Auto Scaling Group creates a new EC2 instance, it automatically tells the Target Group, “Hey, I have a new worker at IP 10.0.1.50!”
  • The target group registers that new IP.
  • The NLB immediately starts sending some of the traffic to that new IP.

3. Why this solves your problem

When your customer’s firewall asks, “Who am I talking to?” it only sees the static IP of the NLB. It never sees the changing IPs of the EC2 instances.

The NLB acts like a shield or a proxy. It receives the traffic on its permanent address and passes it through to the temporary addresses of your servers.

Question 2

Can I use ALB and NLB together?

Yes! AWS allows us to put a Network Load Balancer (NLB) in front of an Application Load Balancer (ALB).

Why?

  1. The NLB provides the static IP your customers need for their firewalls.
  2. The ALB provides smart routing (reading URLs like /users or /login).

So, the traffic flow looks like this: User → NLB (Static IP) → ALB (Smart Routing) → EC2 (The Server)

This sandwich architecture (NLB + ALB) is a pro-level move used by companies that have strict security requirements but still want to use modern, flexible web features.

Real-World Use Case: The B2B Financial Software Company

The company like Stripe, PayPal, or a bank-to-bank payment processor.

These companies provide APIs that other businesses (their customers) use to process money.

  1. The Customer’s Problem: Big banks are incredibly paranoid. Their IT departments will say, “We will only allow our servers to talk to your API if you give us a specific, permanent IP address to put in our firewall.”
  2. The Provider’s Problem: The payment company has a massive website with hundreds of different “paths” (like /process-payment, /check-balance, /refunds). They need an ALB to route these requests to different microservices.
  3. The Solution: They put an NLB at the very front.
  • They give the bank the Static IP of the NLB.
  • The NLB passes the traffic to the ALB.
  • The ALB reads the URL and sends it to the right server.

Conclusion

The network load balancer is the ultimate tool for heavy lifting in the cloud. It doesn’t have the brain to think about reading your web headers that ALB does. But it is capable of handling massive spikes in traffic without breaking a system. If you need to have static IPs, use non-web protocols like UDP, or need to handle millions of connections with zero delay, the NLB is your only choice. It turns a complex network into a fast, reliable, and predictable highway for your data.
