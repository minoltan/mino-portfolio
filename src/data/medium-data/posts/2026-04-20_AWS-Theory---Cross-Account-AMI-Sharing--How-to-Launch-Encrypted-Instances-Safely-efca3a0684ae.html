🌟 Introduction

Sharing resources across AWS accounts is a standard practice for many organizations, but when encryption enters the mix, the workflow becomes a precise multi-step operation. Launching an EC2 instance in a target account from an Amazon Machine Image (AMI) located in a source account requires more than just a simple “share” click if that AMI is encrypted with an AWS KMS key. Because KMS keys are regional and account-bound, you must orchestrate a handshake between the two accounts that involves modifying image permissions, updating key policies, and ensuring the target IAM entity has the exact cryptographic permissions required to “unlock” the image. Understanding this process is not just vital for passing the AWS Solutions Architect exam; it is a critical skill for managing secure, multi-account environments in production.

The Step-by-Step Handshake

To move an encrypted AMI from Account A (Source) to Account B (Target), you must follow this specific sequence:

  1. Modify AMI Launch Permissions: In Account A, you must update the AMI’s properties to add Account B’s ID to the “launch permissions” list. This makes the AMI visible and usable by the target account.
  2. Share the KMS Key: Since the AMI is encrypted, Account B needs permission to use the key in Account A. You must update the KMS Key Policy in Account A to include Account B as a root user or specific IAM role.
  3. Configure Target IAM Permissions: In Account B, the user or role launching the instance must have permissions to call kms:DescribeKey, kms:ReEncrypt*, kms:CreateGrant, and kms:Decrypt.
  4. Launch and (Optional) Re-encrypt: Account B launches the instance. While it can use Account A’s key to run the instance, it is a best practice for Account B to re-encrypt the resulting EBS volumes with its own KMS key for independent management.

Real-World Example: The “Centralized Security” Hub

Imagine a company called “SecureCloud Corp” that maintains a “Golden Image” account. Their security team creates a highly hardened Linux AMI in Account A and encrypts it with a Customer Managed Key (CMK) to ensure it meets compliance standards.

The Scenario: The Development Team in Account B needs to launch their web servers using this hardened “Golden Image.”

The Solution:

  • The Security Team (Account A) adds the Development Account ID to the AMI launch permissions.
  • They update the Key Policy of the “Golden Key” to allow the Development Account to use it.
  • The Dev Team (Account B) uses their IAM role to “pull” the AMI and launch their instances. To ensure they aren’t dependent on the Security Team’s key forever, they choose to re-encrypt the instances’ EBS volumes using their own “Dev-Account-Key” during the launch process. This gives the Dev Team total control over their local data while still benefiting from the security hardening provided by the original image.

🧾 Conclusion

Successfully launching an encrypted EC2 instance across account boundaries is a masterclass in AWS identity and access management. It requires a perfect alignment of AMI visibility, cross-account key trust, and granular IAM permissions to ensure that security is maintained without hindering operational speed. By following this “handshake” protocol, you ensure that your organization’s most sensitive images remain protected by encryption while still being accessible to the teams that need them. As you design your multi-account strategies, remember that in AWS, sharing is not just about visibility — it’s about providing the specific cryptographic “keys to the kingdom” necessary to put those resources to work.
