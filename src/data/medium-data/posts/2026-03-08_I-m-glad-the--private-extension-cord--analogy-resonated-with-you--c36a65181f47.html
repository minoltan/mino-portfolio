It’s the classic trade-off: Site-to-Site VPN is fast to deploy and cost-effective because it uses existing internet paths, whereas AWS Direct Connect is the 'dedicated fiber' option for those who can't afford the unpredictability of the open web.

Regarding your question on routing:

1. The 'Why' Behind BGP (Dynamic Routing)

For most production environments, I recommend Dynamic Routing (BGP).

Automatic Failover: AWS provides two tunnels for every VPN connection for high availability. With BGP, if one tunnel goes down, the traffic automatically reroutes to the second tunnel without manual intervention.

Simplicity at Scale: If you add a new subnet to your office, BGP 'advertises' it to AWS automatically. With static routing, you’d have to manually update the route tables in AWS every time your local network changes.

2. When to Stick with Static

I usually only stick with Static Routing if the on-premises device is an older legacy firewall that doesn't support BGP, or if the network is extremely simple (e.g., a small branch office with a single, unchanging IP range).

The Latency Factor

Since we are 'riding the public internet,' as you mentioned, I always remind people to monitor their MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit) sizes. Because VPNs add an 'encryption header' to every packet, sometimes large packets get dropped or fragmented, leading to that 'slow' feeling even when the bandwidth looks fine!"
