🌟 Introduction

In the theory blog, we discussed the scaling policies. Now, it’s time to see them in action. Imagine you’re running an online ticket office. Everything is quiet until a major concert is announced at 10:00 AM. Within seconds, thousands of fans hit your site. You need your infrastructure to notice this spike and add servers automatically before the site crashes.

In this hands-on guide, we will set up a Target Tracking Policy and use a stress tool to simulate a massive traffic spike.

Step 1: Setting the Scaling Bounds

Before we test, we need to allow the ASG to grow.

  1. Go to your Auto Scaling Group and click Edit.

2. Set the Max capacity to 3. (This ensures the ASG has “room” to add instances during our test).

3. Keep the Desired and Min capacity at 1.

4. Click Update.

Step 2: Create a Target Tracking Policy

Now, let’s give the ASG its “instructions.”

  1. Inside your ASG, click the Automatic scaling tab.

2. Under Dynamic scaling policies, click Create dynamic scaling policy.

3. Policy type: Choose Target tracking scaling.

4. Metric type: Select Average CPU utilization.

5. Target value: Set this to 40. (This tells AWS: “Try to keep the average CPU across all my servers at 40%”).

6. Click Create.

Note: Behind the scenes, AWS just created two CloudWatch Alarms for you. One for “AlarmHigh” (to scale out) and one for “AlarmLow” (to scale in).

Step 3: Simulating the Spike (The “Stress” Test)

We need to make our single instance work hard so the CPU goes above 40%.

  1. Select your running EC2 instance and click Connect using EC2 Instance Connect.
  2. We need to install a utility called stress. Run these commands:
sudo amazon-linux-extras install epel -y
sudo yum install stress -y

3. Now, start the “fire” by running:

stress -c 4

(This command forces the CPU to 100% by running 4 heavy processes.)

Step 4: Watch it Scale

  1. Monitor the Metric: Go back to your ASG and look at the Monitoring tab. Within a few minutes, you’ll see the CPU line skyrocket.
  2. Check the Activity: Switch to the Activity tab. You will soon see a new entry: “Launching a new EC2 instance… due to a target tracking policy.”
  3. Verify: Once the second instance is healthy, your ASG’s Desired Capacity will have moved from 1 to 2 (or even 3).

Step 5: Scaling Back In (Cleanup)

  1. Go back to your terminal and stop the stress test by pressing Ctrl+C.
  2. Wait: Scaling in (removing instances) is always slower than scaling out. This is a safety feature to prevent “flapping” (rapidly adding and removing servers).
  3. After about 15 minutes of low CPU, you will see the ASG Terminate the extra instances and go back down to 1.

Conclusion

You’ve just witnessed the core magic of the cloud. By setting a simple target of 40% CPU, you created a system that monitors itself, responds to heavy load by adding resources, and shrinks back down when the work is done to save you money. For your real-world apps, this means you can sleep soundly knowing your infrastructure is watching your back.
