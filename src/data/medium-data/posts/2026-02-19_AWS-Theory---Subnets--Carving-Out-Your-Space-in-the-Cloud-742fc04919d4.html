🌟 Introduction

Building a VPC is like buying a large plot of land. But you can’t just throw everything into one giant field and expect it to stay organized. To make your network functional, secure, and efficient, you need to divide that land into specific zones. In AWS, these zones are called subnets. By splitting your VPC into smaller sub-ranges of IP addresses, you gain the power to decide which parts of your application are open to the world and which stay locked behind closed doors.

What is a subnet?

A subnet (short for “sub-network”) is a range of IP addresses within your VPC. While the VPC covers the entire network, subnets allow you to group your resources. Such as EC2 instances or databases, based on their security and operational needs.

The “Reserved Five”: Why your math might be wrong

When you create a subnet, you don’t actually get to use every single IP address in that range. AWS automatically reserves five IP addresses for internal networking duties. If you have a CIDR block like $10.0.0.0/24$ (which is 256 addresses), the following are off-limits:

  1. .0: Network Address — The foundation of the range.
  2. .1: VPC Router — The internal “traffic cop” for your network.
  3. .2: Amazon DNS — How your instances resolve web addresses.
  4. .3: Future Use — AWS keeping its options open.
  5. .255: Broadcast Address — Reserved because AWS doesn’t support traditional network broadcasting.
Exam Tip: If you need 29 available IP addresses, a $/27$ (32 IPs) won’t work because $32–5 = 27$. You would need to step up to a $/26$ to ensure you have enough room!

Real-World Analogy: The Hotel Floor Plan

Think of your VPC as a Hotel. The CIDR block is the total number of rooms in the building.

  • The Public Subnet is the Lobby: It’s easily accessible from the street. This is where you put your “receptionist” (web server) to talk to guests (the internet).
  • The Private Subnet is the Guest Rooms: You can’t just walk in from the street. You need a key or a specific internal path to get there. This is where you keep the “valuables” (your databases).

Use Cases: Public vs. Private

The magic of subnets happens when you decide how they talk to the outside world:

  • Public Subnets: These are connected to an Internet Gateway. Use these for resources that must be reached by the public, such as web servers, external load balancers, or gaming servers.
  • Private Subnets: These have no direct path to the internet. These are perfect for backend applications, database clusters, and internal processing engines where security is the top priority.
  • Multi-AZ Deployment: By placing subnets in different Availability Zones (physical data centers), you ensure that if one building loses power, your “hotel” stays open for business in another.

🧾 Conclusion

Subnets are the secret sauce to a professional cloud setup. They allow you to balance the need for global connectivity with the absolute necessity of internal security. By mastering the “Reserved Five” and understanding how to segment your traffic, you’re no longer just launching servers, you’re engineering a robust, secure infrastructure.
