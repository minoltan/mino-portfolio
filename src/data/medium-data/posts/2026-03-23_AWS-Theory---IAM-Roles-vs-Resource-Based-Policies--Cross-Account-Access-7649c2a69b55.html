🌟 Introduction

When building a multi-account AWS architecture, one of the most common challenges is enabling a user in “Account A” to interact with a resource in “Account B.” While both IAM Roles and Resource-Based Policies (like S3 Bucket Policies) can solve this problem, they function in fundamentally different ways. Choosing between them isn’t just a matter of preference; it’s about whether you want your user to “become” someone else or simply “be allowed” inside. Understanding the trade-offs specifically how permissions are retained or surrendered is key to designing a secure and efficient workflow that doesn’t disrupt your existing automation or user access.

The Fundamental Difference

The choice between a role and a policy comes down to identity vs. permission.

1. IAM Roles (The “Hat” Swap)

When you assume an IAM role in another account, you are essentially putting on a new “hat.”

  • Surrender Permissions: The moment you assume the role, you give up your original permissions in Account A. You only possess the permissions granted by the role in Account B.
  • The Workflow: You use the AssumeRole API call to get temporary credentials for Account B.

2. Resource-Based Policies (The “Guest List”)

With a resource-based policy, the resource itself (like an S3 bucket or an SQS queue) has a “guest list” that explicitly mentions your user from Account A.

  • Retain Permissions: You never give up your original identity. You keep your Account A permissions while simultaneously gaining access to the specific resource in Account B.
  • The Workflow: You simply perform the API call directly against the resource in Account B.

Real-World Example: The “Data Migration” Scenario

Imagine you have a developer who needs to scan a DynamoDB table in Account A and upload the results to an S3 bucket in Account B.

  • If you use an IAM Role: The developer assumes a role in Account B to write to S3. Problem: They have now lost their permissions to read from DynamoDB in Account A! They would have to constantly flip back and forth between identities.
  • If you use a Resource-Based Policy: You add a bucket policy to the S3 bucket in Account B that allows the developer’s ARN from Account A to PutObject.
  • Solution: The developer can now read from DynamoDB and write to S3 in a single, seamless operation without ever switching roles.

🧾 Conclusion

In the grand scheme of AWS security, IAM Roles are excellent for granting broad, temporary access to entire accounts, while Resource-Based Policies are the “secret weapon” for surgical, multi-tasking operations. By choosing a resource-based policy, you avoid the administrative overhead of switching identities and allow your users to maintain their local permissions — a necessity for tasks that span across account boundaries. As you scale your organization, remember that the goal is to provide the least privilege necessary with the most operational fluidity.
